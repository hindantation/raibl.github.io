The Algorithm That Rewrote Your Ad

Meta's Advantage+ platform replaced brand-approved creative with bizarre AI-generated alternatives, including an elderly woman promoting activewear, even after advertisers had disabled the feature.

Incident type Automated creative replacement, loss of brand control
Date 2024–2025
Risk Creative control, brand safety, platform governance, advertiser trust

Meta's Advantage+ advertising suite promised marketers something appealing: AI that continuously tests and improves creative performance, automatically generating variations to find what works. For some advertisers, it delivered. For others, it delivered something entirely different.

Reports emerging in 2024 and intensifying through 2025 described a consistent and alarming pattern: Meta's AI was replacing high-performing, brand-approved creative with autonomous alternatives. One activewear brand, True Classic, found that its approved ad imagery had been replaced with an AI-generated image of an elderly woman wearing their clothes.

Others reported surreal visuals: models with unnaturally contorted limbs, flying cars, and imagery with no logical connection to the product being advertised.

The most alarming element was not the quality of the AI output, but the fact that advertisers had turned the feature off.

Multiple brands reported that Meta's automatic creative adjustment settings were re-enabling themselves after being disabled, meaning campaigns were running AI-modified creative without the advertiser's knowledge or consent. The feature appeared to default back to opt-in on a rolling basis.

Meta acknowledged the issue in March 2026, confirming that advertisers who opt out of Advantage+ creative would have that preference saved going forward. The admission implicitly confirmed what advertisers had been reporting for over a year: the platform had been overriding their choices.

The governance question this raises is not unique to Meta. Any AI-powered platform that can autonomously modify what consumers see without the advertiser's real-time knowledge fundamentally changes the risk profile of that platform. Brand safety cannot be assumed; it must be actively audited.

Learning Outcomes

After discussing this case, participants should be able to:

  • Explain why AI-assisted ad platforms require active, ongoing auditing rather than one-time settings configuration.
  • Identify the risks of AI creative automation to brand consistency, tone, and legal compliance.
  • Describe a minimum viable creative governance checklist for brands running campaigns on AI-optimisation platforms.
  • Distinguish between platform performance claims and platform accountability.
  • Assess what recourse a brand has when a platform's AI creates and publishes content the brand did not approve.

Discussion Questions

  • Does your team regularly audit live campaigns to check whether platform AI has modified creative since approval, including on Meta, Google, and TikTok?
  • Who in your organisation is responsible for reviewing platform AI settings when those platforms release updates, and how often does that review happen?
  • If a platform's AI published something off-brand or offensive under your account, what would your immediate response be, and who owns that response?
